Engineering Ethics  ·  MAE 4300

Boeing 737 MAX Ethics Analysis

An ethical analysis of the Boeing 737 MAX crashes through the lens of ASME engineering canons — examining accountability, transparency, organizational culture, and what engineers owe the public when safety and business pressure collide.

Engineering EthicsASME CanonsSafetyAccountability

Central Thesis

The 737 MAX case shows that accountability in engineering must be active, not passive — and that ASME Canon 1 (safety of the public) must override scheduling, cost pressure, and employer loyalty when life is on the line. Transparent communication is part of "safety," not an optional extra.

Introduction

The Boeing 737 MAX crashes are often described as a technical failure, but the ethical failure was broader and harder to pinpoint. Decisions about design, documentation, training, certification, and organizational incentives combined to put the public at risk. The central question is not only what failed, but who had decision and authority, what duties they owed, and how they should have acted when safety concerns surfaced.

Relevant Facts

Technical

  • MCAS design relied on a single AoA sensor — a single-point-of-failure
  • Optional/disabled alerting meant pilots could be unaware of sensor errors
  • Pilot training and MCAS documentation were minimized or omitted
  • Certification shortcuts accelerated time to market

Individual decisions

  • Engineer concerns about single-sensor risk were raised but minimized
  • Test pilot warnings existed and should have triggered deeper review
  • FAA delegation and limited oversight shaped how risk was assessed

Organizational

  • Corporate pressure favored speed-to-market over conservative safety margins
  • Profit-driven training policy left pilots unaware of MCAS behavior
  • Internal culture discouraged engineers from raising safety concerns
  • Boeing's post-crash response deepened public mistrust

Five Core Ethical Issues

1. Cost and schedule vs. testing and validation

Rushing validation to meet market pressure is ethically wrong when it increases foreseeable risk. Canon 1 overrides Canon 4 in life-critical systems.

2. Documentation and transparency about MCAS

Withholding safety-critical system behavior from regulators and pilots violates Canon 7 (truthful communication) and directly contributes to violating Canon 1.

3. Pilot training vs. cost

Minimizing training to reduce cost in a life-critical system is ethically unacceptable. Canon 1 overrides competitive business incentives.

4. Regulatory oversight

Reliance on manufacturer testing alone is ethically wrong when it reduces meaningful oversight. Canon 1 demands independent verification when stakes are catastrophic.

5. Internal objections and whistleblowing

Engineers had an ethical duty to escalate concerns. Employer loyalty cannot outrank public safety. Failure to escalate contributed directly to harm.

Conclusion

The 737 MAX case illustrates how ethical failure can come from a chain of individually "reasonable" business and procedural decisions that together violate the profession's most basic duty. Under ASME ethics, the hierarchy is clear: public safety is paramount, and transparency, training, and independent verification are not optional — they are part of what safety means.
