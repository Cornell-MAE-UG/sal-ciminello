Aerodynamics  ·  MAE 4021 Senior Design

Wind Turbine Senior Design

Aerodynamic design and analysis of a three-bladed horizontal-axis wind turbine using Blade Element Momentum theory and ANSYS CFD. Designed for competitive power coefficient while remaining analytically justified from first principles.

BEM TheoryANSYS FluentMatlabFusion 360Aerodynamics

Project Overview

Completed as part of MAE 4021: Senior Design at Cornell University. The goal was to design a turbine geometry that achieved competitive aerodynamic performance while remaining manufacturable and analytically justifiable using first-principles methods — not a black-box optimization.

Objectives

  • Analyze a given baseline turbine using BEM theory and CFD
  • Develop a blade design methodology including airfoil selection and chord/twist distributions
  • Design and optimize a new rotor via parametric sweeps in Matlab
  • Compare analytical predictions to high-fidelity CFD results

Technical Approach

Aerodynamic Modeling

Blade geometry was designed using BEM theory, combining 2D airfoil performance data with momentum balance along the blade span. This enabled systematic study of chord and twist distributions, tip-speed ratio sensitivity, and local lift/drag contributions.

Computational Analysis

The final blade geometry was imported into ANSYS for CFD simulation to visualize flow behavior, examine pressure and velocity distributions, and compare qualitative trends against BEM predictions.

Design Iteration

Matlab automated parametric sweeps over blade geometry and operating conditions, enabling informed design trade-offs rather than trial-and-error.

CFD visualization showing flow structure and loading trends across the three-bladed turbine geometry.

Results

  • Complete turbine blade design grounded in aerodynamic theory
  • Consistent performance trends between BEM predictions and CFD results
  • Clear insight into how geometric decisions affect efficiency and loading
